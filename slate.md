### 2. Four Core Trends (250–300 words each)

#### **Trend 1: AI Takes Social Engineering to the Next Level**

AI has transformed social engineering from opportunistic to highly targeted. Generative models now enable threat actors to replicate executive voices, faces, and writing styles with precision, blurring the line between authenticity and deception.
The Arup incident, where employees followed instructions from a convincing AI-generated deepfake of a senior leader, underscored this shift. Likewise, Replit’s autonomous AI agent that accidentally deleted production code illustrated the growing need for human oversight over machine-initiated actions.
For organizations, the takeaway is clear: traditional identity verification is no longer sufficient. Trust signals such as voice, video, and written communication can be fabricated. The new control frontier lies in behavioral validation, secondary authorization, and governance structures that ensure AI systems remain supervised by accountable humans.

---

#### **Trend 2: Ransomware… Yes, It’s Still a Threat**

Despite significant investment in resilience, ransomware remains one of the most financially and operationally disruptive attack types. In 2025, threat actors became more selective, focusing on organizations with high operational dependency and reputational exposure.
Coinbase’s insider extortion case and Marks & Spencer’s prolonged outage both demonstrated that ransomware has evolved into a multifaceted extortion model — one that combines data theft, exposure, and reputational coercion.
The shift from encryption to pressure means response strategies must evolve beyond backup restoration. Ransomware today is a brand and stakeholder crisis as much as a technical one, requiring communications, legal, and risk teams to operate in unison.

---

#### **Trend 3: Third-Party Vendor Trust Becoming Riskier**

As organizations expand digital ecosystems, vendor access has become the most exploited attack vector. The TeleMessage breach revealed how backend weaknesses in “secure” communication platforms can expose sensitive archives from government and financial clients.
The incident illustrated a hard truth: a vendor’s controls are an extension of your own. Security oversight, contractual accountability, and independent validation are now essential components of third-party risk management.
Executives should view vendor assessments not as procurement exercises but as security architecture decisions. The strength of an organization’s defense increasingly depends on the hygiene of those it connects to.

---

#### **Trend 4: Our Interdependencies Amplify Cyber Attacks**

The ToolShell SharePoint compromise of July 2025 demonstrated how a single platform vulnerability can cascade across industries. Over 400 organizations — including the U.S. National Nuclear Security Administration — were impacted due to a flaw in a widely deployed collaboration connector.
This incident reflected the growing systemic nature of cyber risk: one weak link in shared infrastructure can create an ecosystem-wide event.
Modern resilience depends not only on internal controls but also on visibility into digital interdependencies. Effective governance now requires organizations to understand where critical business functions intersect with third-party and platform risk — and to plan for failure within those ecosystems.

### 3. Predictions for 2026: Monsters Under the Bed (300–350 words)

> The patterns of 2025 point to three defining priorities for 2026. While technology continues to advance, the human, operational, and governance layers of cybersecurity will determine who withstands the next wave of disruption.

**1. Social Engineering Will Surge as Attackers Outsmart AI Defenses**
As organizations strengthen automated detection, attackers will increasingly use AI to exploit human trust — through voice clones, live impersonations, and tailored manipulation. Social engineering campaigns will become faster, more adaptive, and nearly indistinguishable from legitimate communication.

**2. Ransomware Will Mature into Organized Extortion**
The business model of ransomware is shifting toward multi-layered extortion. Attackers will combine encryption with exposure, legal threats, and stakeholder pressure. The objective will be leverage — not just disruption — forcing leadership teams to weigh financial, regulatory, and reputational consequences simultaneously.

**3. Human Oversight Will Define Responsible AI Governance**
The next major risk frontier will be AI accountability. Incidents such as Replit’s autonomous system failure and Deloitte’s 2025 controversy with the Australian government — where AI-generated deliverables were reportedly used without disclosure — have shown the reputational and compliance risks of unchecked AI.
In 2026, regulators and boards will push for clearer governance frameworks: audit trails for AI decisions, mandatory human validation points, and defined accountability for outcomes produced by machine intelligence.
