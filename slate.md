# When AI Becomes the Risk: How Cyber Insurers Are Rewriting the Rules

Generative AI lawsuits in the United States grew 978% between 2021 and 2025 (FastCompany). That single number explains most of what's happening in the insurance market right now. Insurers spent the last decade learning how to price ransomware and data breaches. AI is a risk they can't measure yet, and when insurers can't measure something, their default move is to narrow what they'll pay for.

For any organization buying or renewing cyber coverage in 2026, the old question, "is AI covered?", isn't useful anymore. The better question is: which AI, in which policy, under what conditions? Here's where things actually stand.

## The exclusion wave is real, but it's landing somewhere specific

You may have seen headlines suggesting insurers are walking away from AI risk altogether. That's not quite right. The pullback is concentrated in a specific set of policies: general liability, director and officer (D&O) coverage, and professional liability (E&O) policies. These are the policies that protect a company's leadership and broad operations, not the dedicated cyber policy itself.

What's happening in that corner of the market:

- **Berkshire Hathaway, Chubb, and Travelers** have all won regulatory approval to remove AI-related damages from corporate policies (Adversa AI).
- **Berkley Insurance** filed an absolute exclusion across D&O, E&O, and fiduciary lines for any use, deployment, or development of AI, full stop (Adversa AI).
- **ISO**, the industry group that writes the standard policy language most carriers build on, issued three new AI exclusion forms in January 2026. Multiple insurers adopted them within weeks (Adversa AI).

Industry commentators are calling this the end of "silent AI." A few years ago, "silent cyber" described how AI-style and cyber-style losses sat quietly inside traditional policies, neither clearly covered nor clearly excluded, until insurers got nervous and drew hard lines (Fenwick). AI is going through the same process now, just faster.

The good news: dedicated **cyber policies are largely the exception** to this retreat. Most cyber insurers are still affirming coverage for AI-driven attacks. Some, like Zurich North America, are actively expanding what they cover to address AI-related regulatory exposure rather than excluding it. But "still covered" doesn't mean "fully covered." Two specific gaps are opening up inside cyber policies themselves.

## Gap 1: Autonomous AI doesn't trigger the coverage the way a hack does

Most cyber policies are built around one clear moment: a system gets broken into, data gets stolen or encrypted, and a loss follows from that. That's the "trigger" that makes the policy pay out.

Agentic AI doesn't fit that pattern. Picture an AI agent that deletes records, changes a database entry, or authorizes a payment it wasn't supposed to make. Nobody hacked anything. There's no intruder. The AI just acted outside the boundaries it was meant to stay inside, and the loss happened anyway. Fitch Ratings' head of cyber risk put it simply: the real danger with agentic AI isn't that it gets hacked, it's that it acts (Risk & Insurance). Researchers at NYU Tandon describe this as a sliding scale, from AI that only drafts text, to AI that prepares actions for a human to approve, up to AI that executes and changes systems entirely on its own. The further up that scale a deployment sits, the less likely a standard, breach-triggered policy responds (Health System CIO).

A few carriers are starting to plug this gap with new, narrower products rather than rewriting their whole policy language:

- **Chubb** now covers some AI-related incidents, but specifically excludes losses that hit many policyholders at the same time, since one flawed AI model used widely could trigger losses everywhere at once (RoughNotes).
- Some insurers introduced **"AI Security Riders"** in 2026: add-ons that require proof of red-teaming (deliberately testing your AI for weaknesses) and documented risk assessments before they'll extend coverage (Compliance Hub).

## Gap 2: Insurers don't want to underwrite a black box

The second gap shows up with hallucinations and bad AI outputs, not technical break-ins.

- **Air Canada** had to honor a refund its chatbot invented out of thin air.
- **Wolf River Electric** sued Google after its AI Overviews feature falsely claimed the company was facing legal trouble, costing it a customer (Marketing AI Institute).

What links these cases is that nobody, including the insurer, can clearly trace how the AI arrived at its answer. That's exactly what underwriters need in order to assess fault and price a policy. As a result:

- Some insurers are simply declining to write coverage for AI-output claims, because they can't reconstruct the AI's reasoning path.
- Lacking basic AI governance, no inventory of which AI tools you use, no risk register, no documented oversight, is now a real reason claims get denied or coverage becomes hard to get at all (Lexology).

## The upside: defensive AI is already earning real discounts

It isn't all retrenchment. Insurers are also rewarding companies that use AI well:

- **86%** of organizations report getting premium discounts or credits for using AI-based security tools that genuinely strengthen their defenses (CSO Online).
- Companies that pair AI-powered threat detection with phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication and endpoint detection and response (EDR) are seeing premium cuts of **20 to 50%** (CSO Online).
- On the flip side, a Delinea survey found **42%** of companies now have AI-related exclusions written into their cyber policies (CSO Online).

In short, the market is pricing two completely different things under the same label, "AI." One is a defensive asset that earns a discount. The other is an unmanaged liability that earns an exclusion. Which bucket your company falls into comes down almost entirely to the documentation you have on hand.

## What this means at your next renewal

The line between "covered" and "denied" no longer depends on whether you use AI. Nearly everyone does now. It depends on whether you can show how it's governed. At renewal, be ready to show:

- A current inventory of the AI tools and models your organization actually uses
- Documented risk assessments completed before any new AI tool or agent went live
- Evidence of adversarial testing or red-teaming for any AI system that can write to, change, or act on production data
- A clear, written answer to where the human checkpoint sits in any AI-driven process

Carriers are asking for this evidence before a claim happens, not after. The gap between organizations that have it ready and those that don't is already showing up in premiums, and in some cases, in whether coverage is offered at all.
