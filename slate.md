Here is a cleaner panel brief focused on the GRC angle and trimmed to the points that are actually worth saying.

Core message for the panel

The right framing is not “AI created a new cyber risk.” It is that advanced AI is compressing the time between vulnerability discovery and weaponization. Anthropic says Claude Mythos Preview found thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities, and the UK AI Security Institute found it could carry out multi-step attack simulations that previously would have taken humans days of work. 

For governance teams, the real issue is speed, not novelty. A model that can identify, reproduce, and chain vulnerabilities faster than human teams can triage them forces a rethink of patching, escalation, and reporting timelines. 

This is also a third-party and supply chain problem, because the affected software includes major operating systems, browsers, and other critical software. That makes vendor oversight, dependency inventory, and secure-by-design procurement part of the response, not an afterthought. 


Talking points for panel members

AI is turning cyber defense into a faster operating model problem.
The business question is no longer just “Are we patched?” It is “How quickly can we detect, validate, remediate, and prove remediation before the attacker moves?” Anthropic’s Project Glasswing announcement and the AISI evaluation both point to a much shorter attack cycle. 

Governance should focus on measurable control outcomes.
Boards should ask for time-to-detect, time-to-triage, time-to-remediate, and time-to-disclose, not just scan counts or policy attestations. That is the practical way to manage AI-accelerated risk. This is an inference from the speed and chaining capabilities described by Anthropic and AISI. 

The basics matter more, not less.
Asset inventory, patch discipline, least privilege, segmentation, logging, and restoration capability remain the best controls. The UK AI Cyber Security Code of Practice explicitly emphasizes human responsibility, asset tracking, supply chain security, testing, monitoring, and maintenance. 

AI governance now sits inside cyber governance.
Organizations need rules for who can use AI agents, what they can touch, what approvals are required, what logs are retained, and how outputs are verified by humans. That lines up with the UK Code’s focus on human responsibility and lifecycle security. 

Third-party risk should be updated for AI-assisted vulnerability discovery.
Ask vendors how they are using AI in development and security testing, what safeguards prevent misuse, and how quickly they can patch at scale. Project Glasswing shows even major technology firms are treating this as an ecosystem-wide issue. 

Reporting timelines may be tighter than response timelines.
For public companies, the SEC requires a material cyber incident to be disclosed on Form 8-K within four business days after the materiality determination. For critical infrastructure, CIRCIA’s proposed framework requires reporting covered incidents no later than 72 hours after the entity reasonably believes the incident occurred. 

EU and UK clients should hear that AI security is becoming a compliance issue, not just a technical one.
Under the EU AI Act, providers of general-purpose AI models with systemic risk face obligations including risk assessment and mitigation, incident reporting, and cybersecurity protections, with those obligations applying from 2 August 2025. The UK Code of Practice similarly sets baseline security expectations across the AI lifecycle. 


A few lines panelists can use

“This is less about a new kind of attack and more about a much faster attack cycle.” 

“The board question is not whether we patch, but whether we can prove we patch quickly enough.” 

“AI makes weak security posture much more expensive.” 

“The response has to combine cyber hygiene, AI governance, and supply chain oversight.” 


Safe answer if asked whether this is hype

The exact scale of any individual claim should be treated carefully, but the direction of travel is credible: Anthropic says Mythos found thousands of vulnerabilities, and the UK AI Security Institute independently observed stronger multi-step offensive performance than earlier models. That is enough to justify stronger controls even without sensationalizing it.
