Cyber Risk and Compliance: Building Resilience Through AI, Data Governance, and Cross-Team Collaboration

Why This Conversation Is Urgent
This session is not about why cyber matters. It is about why firms are failing despite having programs in place.

Decision timelines have collapsed
•	SEC’s 4-day material incident disclosure, UK regulators expecting rapid escalation and justification, EU (DORA/GDPR) reinforcing structured, documented response expectations.
•	The pressure point: Firms must now make defensible decisions with incomplete information, across cyber, legal, compliance, and privacy, in days not weeks.

Regulatory scrutiny has shifted from controls to coordination
Recent enforcement actions point to:
•	Delayed escalation
•	Inconsistent materiality assessments
•	Poor handoffs between cyber, compliance, and legal
Regulators are no longer asking “Did you have controls?” They are asking:
“How did you decide, who was involved, and what information did you rely on?”
AI has accelerated both risk and response
Attackers are using AI to:
•	Craft convincing phishing and impersonation content
•	Speed up reconnaissance and exploit development
•	Target specific people, systems, and processes
Simultaneously, firms are adopting AI internally for threat detection, alert triage, drafting reports and documentation, and streamlining compliance evidence
But the governance hasn’t caught up.
•	Decisions influenced by AI need auditability.
•	Output needs validation and human oversight.
•	There’s confusion around ownership between cyber, compliance, and risk.
This naturally pulls compliance into AI oversight because AI-driven decisions affect regulatory outcomes.
Data governance failures are now visible during incidents
Gaps in inventories or lineage create very real problems mid‑incident:
•	Misjudging what data was exposed
•	Confusion about which regulators or customers must be notified
•	Errors in scope estimation (too narrow or too broad)
In many incidents, the biggest delays come not from containment but from figuring out what data lived where.
•	Data governance has become a real-time dependency in incident response:
•	Teams need quick answers about sensitivity, ownership, and cross-border flows.
Key Discussion Points
Cyber as a Compliance Risk
•	How cybersecurity failures now create direct compliance exposure (regulatory scrutiny, fines, reporting failures).
•	Why cyber, compliance, and risk teams must operate as one governance function, not silos.
•	What “shared accountability” looks like: joint policies, aligned risk appetite, and coordinated escalation.
•	Examples of real-world consequences when cyber and compliance are not aligned (e.g., delayed breach notification because teams disagreed).
AI as a Threat Amplifier AND a Defensive Benefit
•	The ways AI is making attacks more sophisticated (realistic phishing, impersonations, faster reconnaissance). 
•	Practical ways financial institutions are safely using AI today: 
o	Reducing false positives in threat detection
o	Automating evidence collection for compliance
o	Supporting risk scoring and reporting
•	The importance of guardrails: supervised use, transparency, human review, and avoiding over reliance on automated decisions. 
•	The need for governance around AI tools used by employees and vendors.
Data Governance as the Backbone of both Cyber and Compliance
•	Why firms need a strong handle on where sensitive data lives, who accesses it, and how it flows across systems/vendors.
•	How data classification, access reviews, and retention policies reduce risk and simplify compliance obligations.
•	How poor data governance leads to weak breach investigations (e.g., not knowing what was exposed).
•	The shared responsibility between privacy, compliance, and cyber in maintaining good data hygiene.
Third‑Party & Vendor Risk (Including AI Vendors)
•	Why financial institutions must treat vendor security as an extension of their own security posture.
•	New considerations when vendors use AI models or process client data through AI tools.
•	Simple, practical ways to improve vendor oversight without overburdening teams.
What Regulators Expect to See
•	The shift from policy-based oversight (“show me your policy”) to evidence and outcomes (“show me it works”).
•	Why consistent documentation, testing, and traceability across teams matter more than technical complexity.
•	How compliance and cyber can jointly prepare for exams and avoid duplication of effort.
•	Examples of credible evidence: control execution records, logs of decisions, testing results, and remediation tracking.
Building a Strong Cross-Team Operating Model
•	Why resilience comes from collaboration, not tools.
•	How to establish good working rhythms between cyber, compliance, privacy, IT, and legal (e.g., joint risk forums, shared dashboards).
•	How clear roles reduce confusion during incidents or regulatory reviews.
•	How the culture around transparency, timely communication and mutual trust is often the missing piece.
•	Practical steps to improve collaboration (joint training, tabletop exercises, combined reporting).
