**AI and Cyber Insurance: What's Changing**

**Market Context**
- Cyber [Claims Journal](https://www.claimsjournal.com/news/national/2025/11/05/333914.htm) insurance premiums are projected to rise 15% in 2026, with AI named as the primary driver, as AI adoption widens the attack surface and enables more sophisticated threat actors.
- The global cyber insurance market is estimated at $22.2B in 2025, rising to $35.4B by 2030. [Insurance Journal](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/international/2025/12/22/851972.htm)
- Generative AI-related lawsuits in the U.S. grew 978% from 2021 to 2025 [Fast Company](https://www.fastcompany.com/91539281/corporate-insurers-are-starting-to-back-away-from-ai-risk) , putting immediate pressure on carriers to re-examine what they're willing to cover.

**Technical Incidents (Agentic AI Acting Autonomously)**
- Most cyber policies still require a security breach to trigger coverage. An agentic AI loss, where an AI agent modifies records, deletes data, or executes transactions without a breach, falls outside that trigger. The exposure can happen with no hack involved at all. [Healthsystemcio](https://healthsystemcio.com/2026/06/07/agentic-ai-insurance-gap/)
- Traditional cyber policies tend to require a cyber-attack as the trigger. That leaves the no-breach AI error unaddressed, and each policy covers part of the picture while none clearly owns the whole. [Healthsystemcio](https://healthsystemcio.com/2026/06/07/agentic-ai-insurance-gap/)
- In 2026, insurers began introducing "AI Security Riders" - policy addenda that condition coverage on documented AI-specific security controls, including adversarial red-teaming and model-level risk assessments. [Compliancehub](https://compliancehub.wiki/cyber-insurance-ai-security-riders-2026-coverage-requirements/)

**Non-Technical Incidents (Hallucinations, Bad Outputs)**
- Real-world incidents are driving the shift: Air Canada was forced to honor a refund policy invented by its chatbot; Wolf River Electric sued Google after AI Overviews hallucinated non-existent legal troubles causing a customer to cancel a contract. [Marketing AI Institute](https://www.marketingaiinstitute.com/blog/insurers-move-to-exclude-ai-risks)
- Standalone AI liability coverage for hallucination-driven claims addresses financial loss when a third party suffers harm from inaccurate AI output they treated as reliable, but this is a new and still-maturing product category. [Hcpnational](https://hcpnational.com/generative-ai-liability-insurance/)
- Insurers are examining AI governance the same way they examine cybersecurity posture. Companies without AI risk registers or model inventories are viewed as higher risk, and absence of governance can lead to coverage denials or premium increases. [Lexology](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=b76e0dba-d9a8-44f1-9f5d-6fbd0a22f6b6)

**Are Insurers Dropping Coverage? (Yes, but it's specific)**
- Partially true, but the picture is split. Berkshire Hathaway, Chubb, and Travelers secured state regulator approval to strip AI-related damages from corporate policies entirely. Berkley Insurance filed an absolute AI exclusion for D&O, E&O, and fiduciary lines covering "any actual or alleged use, deployment, or development of AI." Cyber lines are a different story - most carriers there are affirmatively covering AI-driven attacks. [Adversa AI](https://adversa.ai/blog/ai-risk-management-insurance-what-the-new-exclusions-mean/)
- Since January 1, 2026, Verisk/ISO exclusion endorsements have allowed carriers to remove generative AI exposure from commercial general liability policies. At least six major carriers have filed their own AI exclusions with state regulators. [Substack](https://insuranceintel.substack.com/p/chubb-is-excluding-the-risk-its-own)
- Chubb covers some AI-related losses but explicitly excludes incidents resulting in "widespread" losses simultaneously, citing concern about systemic, aggregated exposure from a single model failure affecting many clients at once. [The Rough Notes Company](https://roughnotes.com/ai-exclusions/)
- Many carriers are declining to write policies for claims related to AI-generated outputs in cybersecurity and E&O coverage because they cannot track the reasoning path the AI took to reach a result. [CSO Online](https://www.csoonline.com/article/4159292/insurance-carriers-quietly-back-away-from-covering-ai-outputs.html)

**What's Filling the Gap**
- As of early 2026, only a handful of standalone AI liability products exist globally. Armilla and Chaucer launched "Vanguard AI" pairing cyber and tech E&O coverage with a standalone AI liability policy. Testudo, a Lloyd's Lab-backed MGA, launched in January 2026 offering up to $9.25M per insured. Coalition added deepfake response coverage to its cyber policies in December 2025. [Medium](https://medium.com/@purdyhouse/the-first-ai-liability-insurance-product-has-25-million-in-coverage-five-exist-worldwide-575a2903b17b)
- The trajectory mirrors the early days of cyber insurance in the 1990s: major carriers carve the risk out of general policies, forcing it to become a standalone specialty market. Exclusions from standard lines are the precondition for building dedicated AI liability products. [Substack](https://insuranceintel.substack.com/p/berkshire-chubb-and-travelers-are)
