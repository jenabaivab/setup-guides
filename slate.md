**DFSA Cyber Risk Management and Operational Resilience: What to Know**

The Dubai Financial Services Authority (DFSA) requires all authorised firms operating in or from the Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC) to maintain a robust cyber risk management framework under GEN Rule 5.5 of the DFSA Rulebook. The foundational requirement is that each firm implements an appropriate framework to identify and mitigate cyber risks, and to detect, respond to, and recover from cyber incidents. The DFSA does not prescribe a specific framework or standard but expects firms to implement one consistent with the eight G7 Fundamental Elements of Cybersecurity for the Financial Sector. In March 2026, the DFSA also published Consultation Paper No. 170 (CP170), proposing a new operational resilience framework expected to be implemented by end of 2026, with a 24-month transition period following enactment. Together, these frameworks represent a comprehensive and outcome-focused approach to cyber and operational risk governance across the DIFC.

To ensure your firm is ready to comply with the DFSA's requirements, please consider the following:

**Establish a Cyber Risk Management Framework**

»	Establish, document, and maintain a written cyber risk management framework, approved by the governing body, covering identification, assessment, mitigation, detection, response, and recovery across all cyber risks.
»	Align the framework with internationally recognised standards such as NIST, ISO 27001, or the G7 Fundamental Elements of Cybersecurity for the Financial Sector, and review it at least annually or after any significant changes to the threat landscape or the firm's operations.

**Embed Governance and Senior Management Accountability**

»	Ensure board and senior management are actively engaged in the firm's cyber risk posture — not merely informed. The DFSA's thematic reviews have consistently flagged governance as an area where firms underdeliver.
»	Assign clear roles and responsibilities for cybersecurity strategy, implementation, and oversight, with documented accountability at both board and executive levels.
»	Ensure the board receives regular cyber risk reporting, including updates on emerging threats, testing outcomes, third-party exposures, and any remediation activities in progress.
»	Cyber risk management and data protection should be treated as integrated functions. Separate programmes without coordination are flagged as gaps under DIFC Data Protection Law No. 5 of 2020.

**Maintain an ICT Asset Inventory**

»	Identify and maintain a current, comprehensive inventory of all ICT assets — including systems, applications, data repositories, cloud infrastructure, and third-party hosted environments — classified by criticality (Tier 1, 2, or 3).
»	Review and update the inventory at least quarterly, as regulators are actively requesting inventories with evidence of regular reviews during examinations.

**Implement Technical Controls and Cyber Hygiene**

»	Maintain foundational technical controls, including:
o	Up-to-date anti-malware software with regular definition updates
o	User access management and privileged access controls
o	Network security controls and security monitoring procedures
o	Encryption for data in transit and at rest
»	Implement a structured change management process requiring testing and approval before deploying changes to IT systems or networks, and maintain separate environments for development, testing, and production where feasible.
»	Conduct cybersecurity awareness training at least annually, including phishing simulations and role-specific training for ICT staff and senior management.

**Run a Vulnerability Assessment and Penetration Testing Program**

»	Go beyond automated vulnerability scans — the DFSA has explicitly flagged these as insufficient for critical infrastructure on their own.
»	Conduct regular testing across critical systems, applications, and network infrastructure, encompassing vulnerability assessments, penetration testing, and red team exercises where appropriate.
»	Track, prioritise, and remediate all findings in a structured and documented manner. Share results with senior management and relevant oversight functions.

**Strengthen Third-Party Risk Management**

»	Maintain a current register of all third-party ICT service providers supporting critical or important functions and conduct risk-based due diligence before onboarding and throughout the vendor lifecycle.
»	Ensure contracts include clear provisions covering:
o	Security obligations and standards
o	Review and audit rights
o	Incident notification requirements
o	Sub-outsourcing controls
o	Data return and destruction arrangements
»	Firms remain responsible for DFSA compliance obligations regardless of the extent to which functions are outsourced — this includes obligations arising from sub-contracted services.

**Establish Incident Response and Recovery Capabilities**

»	Develop and maintain a written, board-approved cyber incident response plan covering detection, containment, notification, and recovery, reviewed at least annually.
»	Integrate the incident response plan with the firm's broader business continuity and disaster recovery arrangements, and test it regularly through simulations and tabletop exercises.
»	Notify the DFSA as soon as reasonably practicable, and no later than 72 hours, after becoming aware or having information that reasonably suggests a material cyber incident has occurred, using the appropriate form on the DFSA ePortal.

**Participate in Threat Intelligence Sharing**

»	Register with and actively engage the DFSA's Threat Intelligence Platform (TIP) — the first financial services regulator-led cyber threat intelligence platform in the region, available at no cost. The DFSA now treats TIP engagement as a mandatory point of examination, not optional.
»	Use threat intelligence received through TIP to inform risk assessments, control design, and board-level reporting. Participate in DFSA-led simulations to benchmark resilience against current threats.

**Prepare for the Operational Resilience Framework (CP170)**

The DFSA's proposed operational resilience framework under CP170 marks a deliberate shift from process-driven operational risk management toward an outcome-focused assessment of how disruption could harm clients or the wider financial system. The framework is built around five interlinked elements: identifying critical business services, setting impact tolerances, mapping dependencies, scenario testing, and notifying the DFSA of material disruptions.

»	Assess whether any of your business services meet the threshold of criticality — this determination defines the full scope of the firm's obligations under the framework. Firms that conclude none of their services meet the threshold still carry residual obligations and must document that assessment.
»	For services identified as critical, define impact tolerances specifying the maximum acceptable level of disruption in terms of:
o	Duration of the outage or degradation
o	Acceptable levels of data loss
o	Client or broader market impact
»	Map the people, processes, systems, and third-party dependencies underlying each critical business service, with particular attention to shared infrastructure and concentration risks where multiple services depend on the same resource.
»	Conduct scenario testing across severe but plausible disruption scenarios, including simultaneous failures arising from shared dependencies, and maintain documented outcomes for regulatory inspection. The DFSA does not prescribe fixed testing frequencies but expects a clear rationale for the approach taken.
»	Notify the DFSA as soon as practicable where a disruption has breached or come close to breaching a pre-defined impact tolerance.
»	Begin gap analysis and governance planning now — the 24-month transition period will move quickly for firms that have not yet mapped their critical services or defined tolerances.

**FSRA Cyber Risk Management: What to Know**

The Financial Services Regulatory Authority (FSRA) of the Abu Dhabi Global Market (ADGM) announced the implementation of a new Cyber Risk Management Framework on 29 July 2025, applying to all authorised persons and recognised bodies under its supervision. The framework is codified under GEN Rule 3.5 and compliance is required from 31 January 2026, following extensive industry engagement on Consultation Paper No. 3 of 2025. Its central purpose is to enhance cyber resilience across the ADGM financial services sector and align with the UAE government's broader efforts to combat cyber threats and financial crime at a national level. Outsourcing does not reduce responsibility — even where technology, cloud services, or cybersecurity functions are fully outsourced, the regulated entity remains accountable to the FSRA. Sub-outsourcing must also be understood and actively managed.

To ensure your firm is ready to comply with the FSRA's cyber risk requirements, please consider the following:

**Establish a Written Cyber Risk Management Framework**

»	Establish a written cyber risk management framework covering how cyber risks are identified, assessed, managed, monitored, and reported — generic templates or high-level policies without operational detail will not meet expectations.
»	The framework must be specific to the firm's business model and risk profile, approved by the governing body, and actively maintained. It must cover cyber risk appetite, roles and responsibilities, governance and escalation paths, and integration with the firm's overall risk management.
»	Review the framework at least annually and after any significant cyber event or regulatory change, taking account of UAE federal guidance and recognised international standards.

**Embed Governance and Board-Level Accountability**

»	Cybersecurity is now explicitly a board-level responsibility under GEN 3.5. Boards and senior management are expected to:
o	Approve and actively oversee the cyber risk management framework
o	Understand the firm's actual cyber risk exposure, not just receive summary slides
o	Ensure sufficient resources and expertise are allocated
o	Receive regular reporting on threats, incidents, third-party exposures, and remediation activities
»	Delegation is permitted, but accountability is not transferable. Embed cyber risk into the firm's risk appetite statements and internal control frameworks.

**Identify and Protect Information Assets**

»	Maintain a comprehensive and continuously updated inventory of all ICT assets, including software, hardware, cloud infrastructure, data repositories, and third-party hosted environments.
»	Classify assets by criticality and implement appropriate technical and organisational controls to protect them, calibrated to their sensitivity and risk level.
»	Policies and procedures governing asset protection must be reviewed and updated periodically, particularly after significant cyber events or regulatory changes.

**Implement Technical Controls and Cyber Hygiene**

»	Mandate technical safeguards across all key control areas, including:
o	Malware defence and anti-virus controls with regular updates
o	Encryption for data in transit and at rest, applied according to information sensitivity
o	User access and privileged access management
o	Network security monitoring
»	Apply a change management process that formally assesses cyber risks before, during, and after IT system or network changes, with all changes subject to testing and approval.
»	Implement software update management processes that classify patches by criticality and ensure timely application — critical updates should be treated as a priority, not a routine task.

**Conduct Regular Testing and Monitoring**

»	Conduct regular testing of systems and controls, at a minimum including vulnerability assessments and penetration testing of internet-facing systems on an annual basis. More frequent testing may be warranted depending on the firm's risk profile.
»	Implement continuous monitoring to detect anomalous activity and evaluate control effectiveness on an ongoing basis — documentation requirements are not periodic and should be actively maintained.
»	All testing outcomes must be tracked, prioritised, and remediated in a structured and documented manner, with results shared with senior management.

**Manage Third-Party Cyber Risk**

»	Conduct risk-based due diligence on all third-party ICT service providers before onboarding and maintain oversight throughout the vendor lifecycle.
»	Ensure ICT service contracts include minimum provisions covering:
o	Security obligations and standards
o	Review and audit rights
o	Incident notification requirements — providers must notify the firm of all incidents that have or are likely to have a material impact on its operations
o	Sub-outsourcing controls and visibility into the scope of sub-contracted services
o	Data return and destruction arrangements on contract termination
»	Apply adequate controls to sub-contractors engaged by ICT providers. Firms are expected to understand the full chain of dependencies, not just their direct vendors.

**Establish Incident Response and Recovery Capabilities**

»	Establish, maintain, and regularly test a cyber incident response plan that clearly documents roles and responsibilities, escalation procedures, internal and external communication protocols, and recovery and remediation steps.
»	Integrate the plan with the firm's overall crisis management and disaster recovery arrangements.
»	Define materiality criteria for cyber incidents internally, taking into account operational, financial, and reputational impact, as well as whether the incident triggers notification obligations to other regulators.
»	Conduct post-incident reviews after every significant event to identify control gaps and implement measures to prevent recurrence.

**Report Material Cyber Incidents to the FSRA**

»	Notify the FSRA no later than 24 hours after becoming aware that a material cyber incident has occurred — this obligation applies regardless of weekends or public holidays, and even if full details are not yet known at the time of notification.
»	Use the FSRA's updated cyber incident notification template for initial reports; preliminary data is acceptable at this stage. Failure to report within the required timeframe is itself treated as a potential regulatory breach. Maintain a central incident log and ensure all post-incident documentation is audit-ready.

**Stay Current on Cyber Threat Intelligence**

»	Stay informed on emerging cyber threats, FSRA communications and advisories, and relevant industry developments. The FSRA is developing a dedicated Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) Platform to act as a central hub for sector-specific cyber intelligence — firms should plan to engage with it once operational.
»	Use threat intelligence to inform risk assessments, control design, and board reporting on an ongoing basis.
